What Is the Energy Cost Calculator?

The energy cost calculator estimates how much electricity a specific appliance or device costs to run, based on its wattage, how many hours per day you use it, and your local electricity rate. Understanding energy costs at the appliance level helps homeowners identify the biggest electricity drains in their home, compare the running cost of old versus new appliances before replacing them, and make informed decisions about usage habits. Homeowners looking to cut utility bills, renters trying to understand what is driving their electric bill, and anyone comparing an electric appliance to a gas alternative all benefit from this type of calculation. The US average electricity rate is around 16 cents per kilowatt-hour (kWh), but rates vary significantly by state — Hawaii averages over 40 cents while Louisiana sits closer to 10 cents — so entering your actual rate gives the most accurate result.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Appliance wattage — Find the wattage on the device label, manual, or Energy Guide sticker. Common examples: refrigerator 150W, window AC 1,000–1,500W, clothes dryer 5,000W, LED bulb 9W.
  2. Hours per day — Enter how many hours the device runs daily. For intermittent appliances like a dryer, convert weekly usage to a daily average (e.g., 5 hours/week ÷ 7 = 0.71 hours/day).
  3. Days per month — Enter how many days per month the appliance is used. Enter 30 for always-on devices like a refrigerator.
  4. Electricity rate (cents per kWh) — Find your rate on your utility bill, usually listed as the "energy charge" per kWh. Enter it as cents (e.g., 15.8 for $0.158/kWh).

Understanding Your Results

The calculator outputs daily, monthly, and annual energy cost for the appliance. Use these figures to prioritize efficiency upgrades. A result over $20/month for a single device is worth investigating — that appliance is a significant contributor to your bill. Compare the annual running cost of an old appliance against the purchase price of an Energy Star replacement; many refrigerators and air conditioners pay for themselves in 3–5 years of electricity savings. One limitation: wattage on the label is the maximum draw, and many appliances cycle on and off or use variable power, so actual consumption may be 20–40% lower than the calculated figure for cycling devices like refrigerators and HVAC systems.

Example Calculation

A household runs a 1,200-watt window air conditioner for 8 hours per day, 25 days per month during summer, with an electricity rate of 17 cents/kWh. Daily energy use: 1,200W × 8h = 9,600 Wh = 9.6 kWh. Daily cost: 9.6 × $0.17 = $1.63. Monthly cost: $1.63 × 25 = $40.75. Over a 4-month summer season, this single unit costs $163 to run. Replacing it with a 900-watt Energy Star unit saves 25% — about $41 per season, or $410 over 10 years, more than covering the cost difference between models.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which home appliances use the most electricity?

Heating and cooling systems are the largest electricity users in most US homes, typically accounting for 40–50% of the bill. Electric water heaters are second, followed by clothes dryers, refrigerators, and lighting. Electric vehicle charging can rival HVAC costs depending on driving habits. Targeting these high-consumption appliances for efficiency upgrades delivers the biggest bill reductions.

What is a kilowatt-hour and how is it calculated?

A kilowatt-hour (kWh) is the unit of energy on your electricity bill. One kWh equals 1,000 watts of power used for one hour — so a 1,000-watt microwave running for 1 hour uses 1 kWh. Multiply watts by hours, divide by 1,000 to get kWh, then multiply by your rate per kWh to get the cost in dollars.

Does standby power add up to real money?

Yes. The US Department of Energy estimates standby power accounts for 5–10% of a typical home's electricity bill — roughly $100–$200 per year. TVs, gaming consoles, cable boxes, phone chargers left plugged in, and smart devices all draw power when not in active use. Smart power strips and unplugging devices when not in use eliminates this waste entirely.

How do time-of-use electricity rates affect the calculation?

Many utilities offer time-of-use (TOU) pricing where electricity costs more during peak hours (typically 4–9 PM on weekdays) and less during off-peak hours. If your utility has TOU rates, enter the rate that applies during the hours you actually run the appliance. Running your dishwasher or EV charger at midnight instead of 7 PM can cut that appliance's cost by 30–50% on TOU plans.

How do I find my actual electricity rate?

Look at your monthly electricity bill for a line labeled "energy charge," "electricity supply," or "rate per kWh." Divide your total bill by total kWh used for an effective average rate that includes all fees and taxes. The effective rate is often slightly higher than the advertised per-kWh price because of fixed charges and fees.

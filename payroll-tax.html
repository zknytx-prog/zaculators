What Is the Payroll Tax Calculator?

The Payroll Tax Calculator computes the employer and employee share of payroll taxes — the mandatory contributions to Social Security, Medicare, and federal/state unemployment insurance that are due on every dollar of wages paid. Unlike income tax withholding (which varies by employee), payroll taxes follow fixed statutory rates that apply uniformly, making them straightforward to calculate but often misunderstood. Employers frequently underestimate the true cost of an employee because payroll taxes add 7.65% or more on top of gross wages in employer-side obligations alone. Small business owners, HR administrators, independent contractors considering hiring, and anyone running payroll for the first time will find this tool essential for accurately budgeting labor costs and verifying paycheck deductions.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Gross Wages — Total wages paid to the employee before any deductions for the pay period or year.
  2. Pay Period — Weekly, biweekly, semimonthly, or monthly. Affects how wage base limits are reached throughout the year.
  3. Year-to-Date Wages — Important for Social Security, which has a 2024 wage base of $168,600. Once an employee exceeds this, Social Security tax stops for the rest of the year.
  4. State — State Unemployment Insurance (SUI) rates vary widely by state and employer experience rating. Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) is 6% on the first $7,000 of wages, reduced to 0.6% with the state credit.

Understanding Your Results

Payroll taxes split into two sides. The employee side: Social Security at 6.2% (up to $168,600 wage base in 2024), Medicare at 1.45% (no wage base cap), and Additional Medicare Tax at 0.9% on wages above $200,000. The employer side: matching Social Security at 6.2%, matching Medicare at 1.45%, FUTA at 0.6% (on first $7,000), and state SUI (varies by state and employer history, typically 1–5%). Together, employer payroll taxes add approximately 8–10% to the cost of every employee beyond their stated salary. A $60,000 salary costs the employer roughly $64,600–$66,000 in total labor cost when payroll taxes are included, before benefits.

Example Calculation

An employer pays an employee $5,000 biweekly ($130,000 annual salary). Employee payroll taxes: Social Security $310 (6.2%), Medicare $72.50 (1.45%), total employee FICA: $382.50. Employer payroll taxes: matching Social Security $310, matching Medicare $72.50, FUTA $0 (wage base of $7,000 exceeded early in year), SUI varies by state (assume $25 average). Total employer payroll tax per paycheck: ~$407.50. Total labor cost per paycheck: $5,000 + $407.50 = $5,407.50. Annual employer payroll tax burden: approximately $10,595. At $168,600, Social Security stops — employees earning above this threshold see a noticeable increase in net pay mid-year when OASDI withholding ceases.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Social Security wage base for 2024?

The Social Security taxable wage base for 2024 is $168,600. Both the employee (6.2%) and employer (6.2%) stop paying Social Security tax once an employee's wages exceed this threshold in a calendar year. Medicare tax (1.45% each) has no wage base cap and applies to all wages. The Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% applies only to employees earning above $200,000 — employers withhold this but do not pay a matching portion.

Do self-employed people pay more in payroll taxes?

Yes. Self-employed individuals pay the Self-Employment Tax (SE Tax), which is 15.3% on net self-employment income (up to the Social Security wage base) — covering both the employee and employer shares. Above $168,600, SE Tax drops to 2.9% (Medicare only). The IRS allows self-employed individuals to deduct half of SE Tax as an above-the-line deduction on Form 1040, partially offsetting the burden. Quarterly estimated tax payments must include SE Tax alongside income tax.

What is FUTA and who pays it?

FUTA (Federal Unemployment Tax Act) is an employer-only tax used to fund federal unemployment benefits. The rate is 6% on the first $7,000 of each employee's wages, but employers receive a 5.4% credit if they pay state unemployment taxes on time — bringing the effective FUTA rate to 0.6%, or $42 per employee per year maximum. Employees do not pay FUTA. It is deposited quarterly and reported annually on Form 940. States in credit reduction (those that borrowed from the federal unemployment fund) pay a higher effective FUTA rate.

When must payroll taxes be deposited with the IRS?

Deposit schedules depend on your lookback period (total payroll tax liability in the preceding 12 months). Monthly depositors must deposit by the 15th of the following month. Semiweekly depositors must deposit within 3 banking days of paydays falling Wednesday–Friday, or by the following Wednesday for paydays Saturday–Tuesday. New employers default to monthly. Next-day deposit rules apply when single payroll tax liability exceeds $100,000. Late deposits incur penalties of 2–15% depending on how late.

Are payroll taxes the same for all business types?

The federal rates are the same for all business types, but state unemployment (SUI) rates vary by employer experience rating — businesses with more unemployment claims pay higher SUI rates. New businesses are assigned a standard new-employer rate until they accumulate enough history for experience rating. S-corporation owners who are also employees pay payroll taxes on their reasonable compensation (salary) but not on S-corp distributions, which is a key tax planning distinction that the IRS actively monitors.

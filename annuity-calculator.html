What Is the Annuity Calculator?

An annuity is a financial product that pays out a fixed stream of payments over time, either immediately or starting at a future date. Annuity calculators help you estimate either the future value of a series of deposits you make, or the present value of payments you expect to receive. Insurance companies, retirement planners, and individuals saving for a specific goal all rely on annuity math. The two most common types are ordinary annuities — where payments occur at the end of each period — and annuities-due, where payments occur at the beginning. Understanding which type applies to your situation changes the result meaningfully. This calculator lets you plug in your principal, interest rate, and time period to project what your money will grow to or what a future income stream is worth today.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Value 1 (Principal or Payment Amount) — Enter the recurring payment amount or the lump sum you are working with, in dollars.
  2. Value 2 (Interest Rate or Periods) — Enter the annual interest rate as a percentage or the number of payment periods, depending on the calculation mode.
  3. Click Calculate — The result shows the projected future value or present value based on the inputs you provided.

Understanding Your Results

The future value of an annuity tells you how much a series of regular contributions will be worth after a set number of periods, assuming a consistent rate of return. The present value tells you how much a future income stream is worth right now, discounted at a given rate. A higher interest rate increases future value but decreases present value — these two concepts move in opposite directions. Results are estimates based on the figures you enter. Real annuity products include fees, surrender charges, mortality expenses, and tax treatment that can significantly affect actual returns. Use this calculator for ballpark planning, then review any annuity contract carefully with a licensed financial professional before committing.

Example Calculation

Suppose you deposit $500 per month into a fixed annuity earning 4% annually for 20 years. The future value of that ordinary annuity is approximately $183,000. If instead you are evaluating a pension that will pay you $2,000 per month for 15 years, and your discount rate is 5%, the present value of that income stream is about $189,000 — meaning you should be willing to pay up to roughly $189,000 today to receive that stream of payments. These numbers shift considerably with small rate changes, which is why running multiple scenarios is worthwhile before making a retirement income decision.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a fixed and variable annuity?

A fixed annuity guarantees a set interest rate for a defined period, making it predictable and lower-risk. A variable annuity ties returns to underlying investment sub-accounts, so your balance fluctuates with market performance. Fixed annuities are easier to model with a calculator because the rate is known in advance; variable annuity projections require assumptions about average market returns, which introduces more uncertainty.

How does an annuity differ from a savings account for retirement?

Both grow money over time, but annuities are insurance contracts designed to provide income you cannot outlive. A savings account has no guaranteed income feature — you can withdraw freely but may run out of funds. An annuity can include a lifetime income rider that pays as long as you live, regardless of account balance. That longevity protection comes with fees and restrictions that savings accounts do not have.

Are annuity payments taxable?

Generally, yes — the earnings portion of annuity payments is taxable as ordinary income in the US when distributed. If you funded the annuity with after-tax dollars, your original contributions come back tax-free, but growth is taxed. Annuities held inside an IRA or 401(k) are fully taxable on distribution. Tax treatment varies by annuity type and funding source, so consult a tax advisor for your specific situation.

What is the surrender period on an annuity?

Most annuities have a surrender period of 3 to 10 years during which withdrawing more than a specified percentage (often 10% per year) triggers a surrender charge. These charges can start at 7–8% of the withdrawn amount and decrease annually until the period ends. Understanding the surrender schedule is critical before purchasing — this calculator does not model surrender charges, so factor them in separately.

Can I use an annuity calculator to compare two different annuity offers?

Yes. Run the calculation with the rate and term from offer A, note the future or present value, then re-run with offer B's figures. The higher future value (or lower present value cost) generally favors one offer over the other. However, also compare fees, surrender periods, riders, and the financial strength rating of the issuing insurance company — numbers alone do not capture the full picture of an annuity contract.

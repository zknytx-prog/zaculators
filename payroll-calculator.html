What Is the Payroll Calculator?

The Payroll Calculator converts gross pay into net take-home pay by applying federal income tax withholding, Social Security tax, Medicare tax, state income tax, and any voluntary pre-tax deductions such as health insurance premiums or 401(k) contributions. Understanding this conversion is essential for employees budgeting from their paycheck, for small business owners processing payroll manually, and for anyone negotiating a job offer — knowing that a $75,000 salary translates to roughly $4,800–$5,200 per month in take-home pay (depending on state and deductions) is critical context that a gross salary figure alone does not provide. Payroll calculations in the U.S. involve multiple overlapping tax systems, making this calculator a practical tool for anyone who wants clarity before payday.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Gross Pay — Your salary or wages before any deductions. Enter as annual, monthly, biweekly, or weekly depending on your pay frequency.
  2. Pay Frequency — How often you are paid: weekly (52x/year), biweekly (26x), semimonthly (24x), or monthly (12x). This affects per-paycheck withholding calculations.
  3. Filing Status — Single, Married Filing Jointly, or Head of Household. This determines your federal income tax bracket thresholds and standard deduction.
  4. Federal Allowances / W-4 Withholding — Based on your W-4 form. The 2020+ W-4 uses dollar amounts rather than allowances; enter additional withholding if applicable.
  5. State — State income tax rates vary from 0% (Texas, Florida, Nevada) to over 13% (California). Select your state for accurate withholding.
  6. Pre-Tax Deductions — 401(k) contributions, HSA contributions, health/dental/vision premiums paid pre-tax all reduce your taxable income before federal and state tax are applied.

Understanding Your Results

Your net pay is what hits your bank account after all mandatory and voluntary deductions. The mandatory deductions are: federal income tax (7 brackets from 10% to 37% as of 2024), Social Security at 6.2% on wages up to $168,600, Medicare at 1.45% (plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on wages above $200,000 for single filers), and state/local income taxes. Pre-tax deductions — 401(k), FSA, HSA, employer-sponsored insurance — are subtracted from gross pay before federal and state income taxes are applied, reducing your taxable income and thus your tax burden. FICA taxes (Social Security + Medicare) apply to gross pay regardless of most pre-tax deductions. The result is your net pay — which for most W-2 employees is 65–75% of gross pay.

Example Calculation

An employee earns $72,000/year ($2,769.23 biweekly), files Single in Texas (no state income tax), contributes 6% to 401(k) ($166.15/paycheck), and pays $85/paycheck for health insurance pre-tax. Taxable gross after pre-tax deductions: $2,769.23 - $166.15 - $85 = $2,518.08. Federal withholding on $2,518.08 biweekly (annualized = $65,470): approximately $8,300/year or $319/paycheck. FICA on full gross: Social Security $171.69 + Medicare $40.15 = $211.84. Net pay: $2,769.23 - $166.15 - $85 - $319 - $211.84 = $1,987.24 per paycheck, or $4,317/month. Annual take-home: approximately $51,668 on $72,000 gross — a 28.2% effective total deduction rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between gross pay and net pay?

Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions — the number on your offer letter or employment contract. Net pay (take-home pay) is what you actually receive after federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and any voluntary deductions (401k, health insurance) are withheld. For most American workers, net pay is 65–75% of gross pay, depending on income level, filing status, state of residence, and pre-tax benefit elections.

How does contributing to a 401(k) affect my paycheck?

Traditional 401(k) contributions reduce your federal and state taxable income, so your tax withholding decreases — meaning the actual reduction in take-home pay is less than your contribution amount. For example, contributing $200/paycheck at a 22% federal + 5% state marginal rate costs you only $146 in actual take-home reduction (the rest comes from tax savings). The 2024 employee contribution limit is $23,000 ($30,500 if age 50+). Roth 401(k) contributions do not reduce current taxable income but grow tax-free.

Why do I owe taxes when I file my return if my employer withholds?

Withholding is an estimate based on your W-4 information at the time of filing. If you have multiple jobs, significant side income, investment income, or claimed too many allowances, your withholding may fall short of your actual tax liability. Conversely, over-withholding produces a refund. The IRS recommends using the Tax Withholding Estimator (irs.gov) to verify your W-4 elections mid-year and adjust before filing season surprises you with a large bill — plus potential underpayment penalties.

What states have no income tax?

Nine states impose no individual income tax as of 2024: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (taxes only interest/dividends, being phased out), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Residents of these states see notably higher take-home pay compared to high-tax states. California has the highest top marginal rate at 13.3%, followed by Hawaii (11%), New Jersey (10.75%), and Oregon (9.9%). State taxes can account for $3,000–$8,000+ annual difference in take-home pay at median incomes.

Is overtime pay taxed at a higher rate?

Overtime is not taxed at a special higher rate — it is simply added to your regular wages for the pay period, and the combined total is subject to normal progressive tax rates. However, because overtime pushes your single-paycheck income higher, the withholding on that paycheck may be higher as employers annualize each paycheck's wages to estimate your bracket. This can create the appearance of a higher rate. At tax filing, your actual rate is recalculated across all income for the year, and any over-withholding is refunded.

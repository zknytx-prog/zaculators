What Is the Social Security Calculator?

The Social Security Calculator helps you estimate your monthly retirement benefit and compare the financial impact of claiming at different ages. Social Security retirement benefits are one of the most consequential financial decisions most Americans will make, yet the claiming rules are complex enough that many people leave significant lifetime income on the table by defaulting to early claiming without running the numbers. Your benefit is based on your 35 highest-earning years, adjusted for wage inflation, and then reduced or increased depending on when you claim relative to your Full Retirement Age (FRA). The FRA is 67 for anyone born in 1960 or later. This calculator is useful for workers approaching retirement, couples coordinating spousal benefits, and anyone trying to understand the trade-off between smaller monthly checks sooner versus larger checks later.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Estimated monthly benefit at FRA — Find this on your Social Security statement at ssa.gov/myaccount. It reflects your projected benefit if you claim exactly at your Full Retirement Age.
  2. Birth year — Used to determine your FRA. Born 1943–1954: FRA is 66. Born 1955–1959: FRA phases from 66 to 67 in two-month increments. Born 1960 or later: FRA is 67.
  3. Planned claiming age — Enter the age at which you plan to start benefits. You can claim as early as 62 or as late as 70. Each month before or after FRA changes your benefit permanently.

Understanding Your Results

Claiming before FRA permanently reduces your monthly benefit — by up to 30% if you claim at 62 with an FRA of 67. Delaying past FRA earns Delayed Retirement Credits of 8% per year (two-thirds of 1% per month) up to age 70, for a maximum 24% increase above your FRA benefit. The break-even age — where the cumulative total from delaying surpasses early claiming — typically falls between ages 79 and 83 for most people. If you expect to live past that age, delaying is generally the higher lifetime benefit strategy. Spousal benefits can be up to 50% of the higher earner's FRA benefit, and survivor benefits can be up to 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit.

Example Calculation

Suppose your FRA benefit is $2,400/month and your FRA is 67. Claiming at 62 reduces this by 30%, giving you $1,680/month. Waiting until 70 increases it by 24%, giving you $2,976/month. Over 20 years of benefits (ages 70–90), the delay strategy pays out $2,976 × 240 = $714,240 versus $1,680 × 336 months (ages 62–90) = $564,480. The delay strategy produces approximately $150,000 more in lifetime benefits in this scenario, not counting cost-of-living adjustments (COLA), which apply to whichever benefit you receive.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Full Retirement Age and why does it matter?

Full Retirement Age (FRA) is the age at which you receive 100% of your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) — the benefit calculated from your earnings record. Claiming before FRA triggers permanent reductions of 5/9 of 1% per month for the first 36 months early, and 5/12 of 1% per month beyond that. Claiming after FRA earns 8% per year in Delayed Retirement Credits up to age 70. FRA is the pivot point all benefit calculations reference.

How do spousal Social Security benefits work?

A spouse who did not work or had lower lifetime earnings can claim up to 50% of the higher earner's FRA benefit. The spousal benefit is reduced if claimed before the spouse's own FRA. Importantly, the spousal benefit does not increase with Delayed Retirement Credits — there is no advantage to the lower earner waiting past their own FRA to claim spousal benefits. Divorced spouses who were married at least 10 years may also qualify for spousal benefits without affecting the ex-spouse's benefit.

What is the Social Security earnings test?

If you claim benefits before FRA and continue working, Social Security withholds $1 in benefits for every $2 you earn above the annual limit ($22,320 in 2024). In the year you reach FRA, the limit is higher ($59,520) and withholding drops to $1 for every $3 over the limit. After FRA, there is no earnings test — you can work and collect full benefits simultaneously. Withheld benefits are not lost; they are credited back to you as a higher monthly benefit after FRA.

Are Social Security benefits taxable?

Up to 85% of your Social Security benefit may be subject to federal income tax if your combined income (adjusted gross income + nontaxable interest + half of Social Security) exceeds $34,000 for single filers or $44,000 for married filing jointly. At lower income levels, 0% to 50% may be taxable. Most states do not tax Social Security, but 12 states do have their own Social Security taxes as of 2024.

Should I always wait until 70 to maximize my benefit?

Not necessarily. Delaying is the higher lifetime benefit strategy only if you live long enough past the break-even age (typically 79–83). If you have serious health concerns, need income to cover living expenses, have a lower-earning spouse who would claim spousal benefits sooner, or face other cash flow needs, earlier claiming may be the better practical choice. A financial planner or SSA representative can help model your specific situation.

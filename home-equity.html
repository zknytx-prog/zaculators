What Is the Home Equity Calculator?

The Home Equity Calculator determines how much equity you have built in your home by subtracting your outstanding mortgage balance from the property's current market value. Equity represents your ownership stake — the portion of the home's value you actually own free of lender claims. Homeowners use this figure when deciding whether to pursue a home equity loan, a home equity line of credit (HELOC), or a cash-out refinance. Lenders typically require you to retain at least 15–20% equity after borrowing, so knowing your current equity position tells you the maximum additional capital you could access. Equity also grows through principal paydown, home appreciation, and improvements that raise market value.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Current home value — Enter the estimated market value of your home based on a recent appraisal, comparable sales, or a real estate agent's opinion.
  2. Outstanding mortgage balance — Enter the current payoff amount from your most recent mortgage statement, not the original loan amount.
  3. Click Calculate — The result shows your dollar equity, your equity percentage, and the estimated borrowable equity after the lender's required retention threshold.

Understanding Your Results

Your equity percentage is calculated as (home value minus mortgage balance) divided by home value, expressed as a percent. A result of 20% or above generally qualifies you for a home equity loan or HELOC with favorable terms. Below 20%, lenders may still lend but often at higher rates or with added requirements. If your equity is below zero — meaning the mortgage balance exceeds the home's value — you are "underwater" or in a negative-equity position, which restricts refinancing and sale options. Your loanable equity is typically capped at 80–85% of home value minus the existing mortgage balance, which is the combined loan-to-value (CLTV) limit most lenders enforce.

Example Calculation

A homeowner bought a property five years ago for $320,000 with a $256,000 mortgage (80% LTV). Today the home is worth $390,000 and the mortgage balance is $228,000 after five years of payments. Equity = $390,000 − $228,000 = $162,000, an equity percentage of 41.5%. At an 80% CLTV cap, the lender allows a combined mortgage+HELOC balance of $312,000. Subtracting the existing $228,000 balance, the homeowner can borrow up to $84,000 through a HELOC or home equity loan without triggering additional requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a home equity loan and a HELOC?

A home equity loan disburses a lump sum at a fixed interest rate with equal monthly payments over a set term, similar to a second mortgage. A HELOC is a revolving credit line with a variable rate — you draw funds as needed during a draw period (typically 10 years) and repay during a repayment period. Home equity loans suit single large expenses like a renovation; HELOCs suit ongoing or uncertain-cost projects where you want flexible access to capital.

How does a cash-out refinance differ from a home equity loan?

A cash-out refinance replaces your existing first mortgage with a new, larger mortgage and gives you the difference in cash. It produces a single monthly payment at the new rate. A home equity loan keeps your existing mortgage intact and adds a second loan with its own payment. Cash-out refis are preferable when current rates are lower than your existing mortgage rate; home equity loans make more sense when you want to preserve a low existing rate.

Is the interest on a home equity loan tax deductible?

Under current IRS rules (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017), interest on home equity debt is deductible only if the proceeds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home securing the loan. If you use a HELOC to consolidate credit card debt or pay tuition, the interest is not deductible. Always consult a tax professional to confirm deductibility based on your specific use of funds and filing situation.

How quickly does home equity grow?

Equity grows through three mechanisms: principal paydown from monthly mortgage payments, appreciation in the home's market value, and improvement projects that increase value. In the early years of a standard amortizing mortgage, most of each payment covers interest so principal reduction is slow — a 30-year $300,000 loan at 7% builds only about $4,000 in equity through payments during year one. Appreciation is often the faster builder; a 4% annual appreciation rate on a $350,000 home adds $14,000 in equity per year independent of payments.

What risks come with borrowing against home equity?

The primary risk is foreclosure — home equity loans and HELOCs are secured by your property, so defaulting can result in losing your home. A secondary risk is market value decline: if home prices fall after you borrow, you could go underwater. Variable-rate HELOCs also carry payment-increase risk if interest rates rise during the draw period. For these reasons, financial advisors generally recommend using home equity for value-adding investments like renovations or education rather than for discretionary consumption.

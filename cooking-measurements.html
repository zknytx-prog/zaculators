Frequently Asked Questions

Are grams and milliliters always equal?

No. This page uses a water-style estimate where 1 mL is treated like 1 g for quick kitchen math.

Why can flour and sugar differ from water?

Dry ingredients pack differently and have different densities, so the gram weight changes by ingredient.

Should I rely on cups or grams for baking?

Grams are usually more consistent for baking because scoop style and cup packing can vary.

Why include tablespoons and teaspoons?

Those units are common in everyday cooking and recipe cards, especially for small ingredient amounts.

Can I use this for meal prep scaling?

Yes. It is handy for doubling, halving, and converting recipe liquids and approximate ingredient amounts.

When should I switch to an ingredient-specific chart?

Use one when precision matters, especially for flour, sweeteners, butter, and specialty powders.

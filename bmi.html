What to do next

  1. Treat the result as a starting point, not a verdict.
  2. Pair BMI with waist measures, body composition, and clinical context.
  3. Check trends over time instead of obsessing over one number.
  4. Use realistic habits for weight change, not crash plans.
  5. Talk with a qualified professional for personal guidance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is BMI body fat percentage?

No. BMI is a screening measure based on height and weight.

Can muscular people have a high BMI?

Yes. BMI does not distinguish muscle from body fat.

Is BMI useful at all?

Yes, as a broad population-level screening tool and a simple starting point.

Should I use this for medical diagnosis?

No. A clinician should interpret it in context.

Why use pounds and inches here?

This version is built for a quick US-style calculation.

Can I track changes over time?

Yes, but trends matter more than one isolated reading.

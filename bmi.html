Understanding Your BMI Results

What the Four BMI Categories Mean

Body mass index groups people into four internationally recognized ranges based on their height-to-weight ratio. The World Health Organization and the CDC use the same thresholds for adults:

  • Underweight (below 18.5) — Weight may be insufficient for optimal health. Common causes include restrictive eating, illness, or high activity levels with inadequate caloric intake. Medical evaluation is recommended.
  • Healthy weight (18.5–24.9) — Associated with the lowest statistical risk for weight-related conditions at the population level. Individual health still depends on diet quality, exercise, and other factors.
  • Overweight (25–29.9) — Indicates above-average weight relative to height. Not a diagnosis on its own, but may warrant a lifestyle discussion with a healthcare provider, particularly if waist circumference is also elevated.
  • Obesity (30 and above) — Subdivided into Class I (30–34.9), Class II (35–39.9), and Class III (40+). Higher classes are associated with increased risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and joint problems.

The Muscle-Mass Limitation

BMI is calculated from height and weight alone — it cannot determine whether that weight comes from muscle, fat, water, or bone. This creates a well-known limitation for certain populations.

A professional athlete or bodybuilder may score 27–30 on the BMI scale while carrying very little excess body fat. Conversely, an older, sedentary person may show a "normal" BMI of 23 while carrying significant visceral fat (the metabolically active fat stored around internal organs). For this reason, clinicians frequently pair BMI with waist circumference measurements, DEXA scans, or bioelectrical impedance analysis for a more complete picture.

How to Calculate BMI Manually

The imperial formula is: BMI = (weight in lbs × 703) ÷ (height in inches)²

Example: A person weighing 165 lb who stands 5 feet 10 inches (70 inches total): 165 × 703 = 115,995 ÷ (70 × 70 = 4,900) = BMI 23.7 — squarely in the healthy weight range.

The metric formula is: BMI = weight in kg ÷ (height in meters)²

Frequently Asked Questions

Is BMI an accurate measure of body fat?

No. BMI is a simple screening tool derived from height and weight. It correlates with body fat at the population level but cannot distinguish between muscle mass and fat in an individual. Use it as a starting point, not a definitive measurement.

Can muscular people have a high BMI and still be healthy?

Yes. Athletes and highly muscular individuals often register in the overweight or obese range despite low body-fat percentages. Other measurements like waist circumference or body composition analysis provide a fuller picture.

Is BMI the same for men and women?

The formula is the same, but women naturally carry a higher percentage of body fat at the same BMI value as men. Some researchers argue separate male/female cut-offs would be more accurate, though the standard WHO thresholds are currently applied universally to adults.

Should I use BMI for medical decisions?

No. Treat BMI as a population-level screening tool only. A qualified clinician will interpret your BMI alongside age, sex, ethnicity, medical history, and other clinical data before drawing any conclusions or recommending treatment.

How often should I check my BMI?

Trends matter more than single readings. Checking quarterly or semi-annually while monitoring diet and exercise habits gives you more meaningful data. Consistent changes in BMI over time are worth discussing with a healthcare provider.

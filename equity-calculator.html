What Is the Home Equity Calculator?

A home equity calculator shows how much of your home's value you actually own outright — the difference between the current market value of your property and the outstanding balance on your mortgage. Home equity is one of the most significant sources of household wealth in the United States, and knowing your current equity position matters for several key decisions: qualifying for a home equity loan or HELOC, determining whether you can drop private mortgage insurance (PMI), evaluating whether to sell or refinance, and understanding your net worth. Homeowners approaching 20% equity, those considering cash-out refinancing, and anyone preparing to sell all benefit from running this calculation regularly. Equity grows through two forces working together — your monthly mortgage payments reducing the principal balance, and home price appreciation increasing the property's market value.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Current home value — Enter your home's estimated current market value. Use a recent appraisal, Zillow/Redfin estimate, or comparable sales in your area as a reference.
  2. Remaining mortgage balance — Enter the current payoff balance on your mortgage, found on your most recent statement or lender's online portal.
  3. Second mortgage or HELOC balance — If applicable, enter any additional liens against the property. These reduce your available equity.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator outputs your total equity in dollars and as a percentage of home value (your loan-to-value ratio, or LTV). Under 80% LTV (over 20% equity) is the threshold where PMI drops off on conventional loans and where home equity lending options open up. Most lenders allow you to borrow up to 80–85% of your home's value combined across all loans — your primary mortgage plus any HELOC or equity loan. Subtract your mortgage balance from 80% of your home's value to find your borrowable equity. Keep in mind that online home value estimates can be off by 5–10%; for major financial decisions, a professional appraisal ($300–$600) gives a more reliable number.

Example Calculation

A homeowner bought her house 6 years ago and has paid the balance down from $280,000 to $241,000. Her home is now worth approximately $365,000 based on recent comparable sales. Her equity: $365,000 − $241,000 = $124,000, or 33.9% of home value. Her LTV is 66.1%, well below 80%. She can potentially borrow up to 80% × $365,000 = $292,000 combined — meaning up to $51,000 on a HELOC ($292,000 − $241,000) without going over the 80% LTV threshold most lenders require.

Frequently Asked Questions

How quickly does home equity build?

In the early years of a 30-year mortgage, most of each payment goes to interest rather than principal — equity builds slowly through payments alone. A $350,000 loan at 7% builds only about $3,500 in principal equity in year one. Home price appreciation accelerates equity growth significantly. Markets that gained 5–8% annually in recent years created more equity per year than mortgage payments alone would in a decade.

What is the difference between a home equity loan and a HELOC?

A home equity loan gives you a lump sum at a fixed interest rate, repaid over a set term — similar to a second mortgage. A HELOC (home equity line of credit) is a revolving credit line with a variable rate; you draw what you need, repay it, and borrow again during the draw period (typically 10 years). HELOCs are more flexible but carry rate risk; home equity loans give payment predictability.

At what equity percentage can I remove PMI?

For conventional loans, PMI is required when LTV exceeds 80%. By law (Homeowners Protection Act), lenders must automatically cancel PMI when your LTV reaches 78% based on original amortization schedule. You can request removal at 80% LTV based on current value — potentially requiring a new appraisal. FHA loans have mortgage insurance for the life of the loan regardless of equity, unless refinanced into a conventional loan.

Is tapping home equity a good idea for home improvements?

Home improvements that increase the property's value — kitchen remodels, bathroom updates, roof replacement — can make equity borrowing productive because you are reinvesting in the asset securing the loan. Projects that do not add value (luxury items, non-structural upgrades) are riskier choices. Kitchen remodels recoup an average of 60–80% of cost at resale; adding a deck recouped about 65% in recent surveys.

Can I lose my home equity if property values fall?

Yes. Home equity is not locked in — if market values drop, equity shrinks dollar-for-dollar. Homeowners who bought near the peak of a market cycle with minimal down payments can go "underwater" (owing more than the home is worth) if values fall significantly. Maintaining a reasonable equity cushion — staying well below 80% LTV — provides a buffer against value declines and keeps refinancing options open.

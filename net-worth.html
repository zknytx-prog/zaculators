What Is the Net Worth Calculator?

Net worth is the single most comprehensive measure of your financial health — it captures everything you own minus everything you owe at a given point in time. The Net Worth Calculator adds up your assets (cash, investments, retirement accounts, real estate, vehicles, and other valuables) and subtracts your liabilities (mortgage balance, auto loans, student loans, credit card debt, and other obligations) to produce your personal balance sheet. Financial planners use net worth as a baseline metric because it reflects the cumulative result of every earning, spending, saving, and investing decision you have made. Tracking it quarterly or annually lets you see whether your financial trajectory is moving in the right direction, even in years when income feels stagnant. The Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances shows that median U.S. household net worth was $192,700 in 2022 — but averages vary dramatically by age and income level.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Cash & Savings — Checking accounts, savings accounts, money market funds, and physical cash. Use current balances, not averages.
  2. Investments — Taxable brokerage accounts, stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs. Use current market value.
  3. Retirement Accounts — 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, pension cash value. Use the current vested balance shown on your last statement.
  4. Real Estate — Market value of your home or investment properties. Use a current Zillow estimate or recent comparable sales, not the purchase price.
  5. Vehicles — Current resale value (Kelley Blue Book), not what you paid.
  6. Other Assets — Business ownership stake, life insurance cash value, collectibles, jewelry. Be conservative on hard-to-value items.
  7. Liabilities — Enter all outstanding balances: mortgage, HELOCs, auto loans, student loans, credit cards, personal loans, medical debt.

Understanding Your Results

A positive net worth means your assets exceed your debts — a foundational sign of financial stability. A negative net worth is common early in life, particularly for recent graduates with student loans and no significant assets yet. What matters most is the trend: is net worth growing each year? Age-based benchmarks are helpful guides. Fidelity suggests having 1x your salary saved by 30, 3x by 40, 6x by 50, and 8x by 60. Net worth naturally accelerates as debts are retired and compound investment returns build — so growth in your 40s and 50s often outpaces growth in your 20s and 30s even on the same income. Note that illiquid assets like real estate and retirement accounts are part of net worth but not immediately accessible — liquidity matters separately from total wealth.

Example Calculation

A 38-year-old professional has: checking/savings $22,000, brokerage account $48,000, 401(k) $95,000, home value $380,000, car value $18,000. Total assets: $563,000. Liabilities: mortgage balance $285,000, auto loan $9,500, student loans $14,200, credit card balance $2,800. Total liabilities: $311,500. Net worth: $563,000 - $311,500 = $251,500. With a $110,000 salary, this is 2.3x income — on track by Fidelity's 3x-by-40 benchmark but with room to accelerate debt paydown and investment contributions to close the gap in two years.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I calculate my net worth?

Quarterly or semi-annually is ideal for most people — frequent enough to spot trends but not so often that short-term market fluctuations cause unnecessary anxiety. Many financial planners recommend doing a full net worth calculation on January 1st each year as part of an annual financial review. The goal is tracking direction over time, not obsessing over monthly swings driven by stock market volatility or seasonal cash flow changes.

Should I include my primary home in net worth?

Yes — your home's market value (minus the mortgage balance) is equity and counts as net worth. However, some financial planners calculate net worth both with and without home equity, since a house is illiquid and you need to live somewhere. If your home represents more than 50% of your net worth, you may be over-concentrated in a single illiquid asset. Diversifying into financial assets over time improves both liquidity and risk balance in your overall portfolio.

What is a good net worth by age?

Federal Reserve data shows median net worth by age group: under 35: $39,000; 35–44: $135,000; 45–54: $247,000; 55–64: $365,000; 65–74: $410,000. These are medians — half of households fall below these figures. The Fidelity multiplier approach (1x salary at 30, 3x at 40, 6x at 50, 8x at 60) is a more actionable target tied to your personal income rather than population averages.

Does net worth include retirement accounts?

Yes, retirement accounts (401k, IRA, Roth IRA) are assets and count toward net worth at their current vested balance. For a Roth IRA, the full balance counts since withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. For traditional 401(k) and IRA accounts, some planners apply a tax haircut (20–30%) to reflect future taxes owed upon withdrawal, giving a more conservative after-tax net worth figure. Both approaches are valid — just be consistent when tracking over time.

What is the fastest way to increase net worth?

Net worth grows by either increasing assets or reducing liabilities — ideally both simultaneously. The highest-impact levers are: maximizing retirement contributions (especially to capture employer matches, which are instant 50–100% returns), paying off high-interest debt (eliminating a 22% APR credit card is equivalent to a guaranteed 22% investment return), and avoiding lifestyle inflation as income rises. Automating savings so that raises flow directly into investments rather than spending is one of the most reliable long-term wealth-building habits.

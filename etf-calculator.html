What Is the ETF Calculator?

An ETF (exchange-traded fund) calculator projects how an investment in an ETF grows over time, factoring in your initial investment, regular contributions, expected annual return, and the expense ratio that the fund charges each year. ETFs are baskets of securities — stocks, bonds, or other assets — that trade on exchanges like individual stocks. They have become the dominant investment vehicle for long-term investors because they offer instant diversification, low costs, and tax efficiency. The calculator matters because even a small difference in expense ratio has a large compounding effect over decades: a 0.03% expense ratio (like Vanguard's VTI) versus a 0.75% ratio costs tens of thousands of dollars over a 30-year investment horizon. By modeling different funds, contribution amounts, and time horizons, this tool makes the real cost of fees visible and helps investors make smarter fund selection decisions.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Initial investment — Enter the lump sum you plan to invest today. Even $1,000 makes a meaningful difference when compounded over decades.
  2. Monthly contribution — Enter how much you will add each month. Consistent contributions are often more impactful than the initial lump sum over long periods.
  3. Expected annual return — Enter your assumed average annual return. The S&P 500 has averaged approximately 10% annually before inflation over the past 50 years; use 7% for a conservative inflation-adjusted estimate.
  4. Expense ratio — Enter the fund's annual expense ratio as a percentage. Find this on the ETF's fact sheet or at the fund provider's website. Broad index ETFs often charge 0.03–0.20%.
  5. Investment period — Enter how many years you plan to hold the investment before drawing it down.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator outputs your projected portfolio value at the end of the period, total contributions made, and total gains earned. The most important insight is the difference in final value between a low-cost index ETF and a higher-cost actively managed fund earning the same gross return. The expense ratio is deducted from returns daily; it silently compounds in reverse. Also note that the calculator uses a fixed annual return — real markets fluctuate significantly year to year, and sequence of returns risk (getting bad years early in retirement) matters a great deal in practice. Treat the output as a planning estimate, not a guarantee.

Example Calculation

Two investors each start with $10,000 and contribute $400/month for 30 years, both earning 9% gross annual return. Investor A uses an ETF with a 0.04% expense ratio (net return: 8.96%). Investor B uses an actively managed fund with a 0.85% expense ratio (net return: 8.15%). After 30 years, Investor A has approximately $648,000; Investor B has approximately $573,000. The $75,000 difference is entirely attributable to the expense ratio gap — that 0.81% annual fee cost Investor B more than $75,000 over three decades on the same gross return.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good expense ratio for an ETF?

Broad US stock market index ETFs from Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab charge 0.03–0.05% annually — essentially free. International index ETFs run 0.05–0.15%. Actively managed ETFs or niche thematic funds often charge 0.50–1.00% or more. As a rule of thumb, any ETF charging over 0.50% deserves scrutiny — you need strong evidence of consistent outperformance to justify the extra cost, which most actively managed funds fail to deliver long-term.

How is an ETF different from a mutual fund?

ETFs trade on stock exchanges throughout the day at market prices, while mutual funds price once per day at net asset value (NAV) after the market closes. ETFs are generally more tax-efficient because their structure minimizes capital gains distributions. Most ETFs also have lower minimum investments — you can buy a single share — while many mutual funds require $1,000–$3,000 minimums. Index ETFs and index mutual funds tracking the same benchmark have nearly identical returns.

Should I invest in a broad market ETF or sector ETFs?

Broad market ETFs like VTI (total US market) or SPY (S&P 500) give you exposure to hundreds or thousands of companies in one fund, reducing single-sector risk. Sector ETFs — technology, healthcare, energy — let you overweight specific areas but increase concentration risk. Research consistently shows that most investors who tilt toward specific sectors underperform the broad market over 10+ years. Broad index funds are the evidence-backed starting point for most long-term investors.

What return rate should I use in the ETF calculator?

For a broad US stock market ETF, the historical average annual return has been approximately 10% nominal and 7% inflation-adjusted over 50+ years. For planning purposes, using 7–8% nominal is a reasonable middle-ground assumption. Bond ETFs have historically returned 3–5%. A balanced portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds) might use 6–7%. Avoid using recent 3–5 year returns as your assumption — short-term performance is not predictive of long-term results.

Are ETF dividends automatically reinvested?

Not automatically — unlike mutual funds with automatic DRIP, ETF dividends are typically paid as cash to your brokerage account. Most brokerages offer a DRIP option for ETFs that automatically reinvests dividends in additional shares at no commission. Enabling DRIP is highly recommended for long-term investors because reinvested dividends have historically accounted for a significant portion of total stock market returns over multi-decade periods.

What Is the Appliance Cost Calculator?

The Appliance Cost Calculator helps you figure out how much a household appliance costs to run over time by combining its power draw, how often you use it, and your local electricity rate. Most people have no idea that a clothes dryer running 45 minutes per load, five loads a week, can add $15–$25 to their monthly electric bill — or that an older refrigerator from 2005 may use three times as much electricity as a new ENERGY STAR model. This calculator makes those hidden costs visible. Homeowners use it when deciding whether to replace an aging appliance, renters use it to estimate utility costs in a new unit, and energy-conscious households use it to identify which devices are the biggest electricity hogs and where cutting usage will save the most money.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Value 1 (Wattage) — Enter the appliance's power consumption in watts. You can find this on the nameplate label on the back or bottom of the appliance, or in the owner's manual. Common values: a microwave is roughly 1,000–1,200 W, a window AC unit is 500–1,500 W, a standard refrigerator is 100–400 W.
  2. Value 2 (Hours per day or rate) — Enter how many hours per day you run the appliance, or your electricity cost in cents per kilowatt-hour (kWh). The national US average is around 16–17 cents per kWh as of 2025, though rates vary widely by state.
  3. Click Calculate — The result shows estimated daily, monthly, or annual electricity cost for that appliance.

Understanding Your Results

Electricity cost is calculated as: (Watts ÷ 1,000) × Hours × Rate per kWh = Cost. A 1,500-watt space heater running 8 hours a day at $0.16/kWh costs about $1.92 per day, $57.60 per month. The result you see is an estimate based on constant usage — real appliances cycle on and off, have variable loads, and may use standby power even when "off." Factor in a 10–20% buffer for real-world variation. The calculation also does not include the cost of the appliance itself, installation, or maintenance — it is purely the electricity operating cost. Use the monthly or annual figures when comparing two appliances to determine the payback period of upgrading to a more efficient model.

Example Calculation

A 250-watt chest freezer runs continuously (24 hours/day). At $0.16 per kWh, the daily cost is: (250 ÷ 1,000) × 24 × $0.16 = $0.96/day, or about $28.80/month — $345.60/year. A newer ENERGY STAR chest freezer of similar size might draw only 120 watts, cutting that cost to roughly $166/year — a savings of about $180 annually. If the new model costs $400, it pays for itself in roughly 2.2 years through electricity savings alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where do I find the wattage of my appliance?

Check the nameplate label on the back, bottom, or side of the appliance — it usually lists volts and amps or directly states watts. If you see volts and amps but not watts, multiply them together (Watts = Volts × Amps). The owner's manual or manufacturer's website also lists rated power consumption. For variable-load devices like refrigerators, use the average wattage rather than the peak startup draw.

What is the average electricity rate in the US?

The US Energy Information Administration reported a national residential average of about 16–17 cents per kWh in 2024–2025. However, rates vary significantly: Hawaii averages over 40 cents, while Louisiana and Oklahoma average under 12 cents. Check your most recent utility bill — the rate is usually listed as cents per kWh in the billing detail section — and use that number for the most accurate result.

How do I calculate payback period when replacing an old appliance?

Run the calculator twice: once with the old appliance's wattage, once with the new model's wattage. Subtract the annual costs to find the yearly savings. Divide the purchase price of the new appliance by the annual savings to get the payback period in years. For example, $300 savings ÷ $180 annual savings = 1.67 years payback. After that break-even point, the new appliance is saving you money every year.

Does standby or phantom power usage add up significantly?

Yes. The US Department of Energy estimates standby power accounts for 5–10% of a typical home's electricity bill. A TV on standby may draw 1–5 watts constantly; a cable box might draw 15–20 watts even when not in use. Over a year, these small draws add up to tens of dollars per device. Smart power strips and unplugging devices when not in use can meaningfully reduce this phantom load.

Which household appliances typically cost the most to run?

Central air conditioning and electric heat are typically the top electricity consumers, often accounting for 40–50% of a home's total usage. Electric water heaters, clothes dryers, and electric ovens also rank high. Refrigerators run constantly but at lower wattage, making them a moderate cost. Lighting and small electronics are comparatively minor in most households, especially with LED bulbs replacing incandescent fixtures.

What Is the Home Affordability Calculator?

The Home Affordability Calculator determines how much house you can realistically purchase based on your income, existing debt, down payment savings, and the current interest rate environment. Rather than guessing at a price range, this tool applies the same two ratios mortgage underwriters use — the front-end ratio (housing cost as a percentage of gross income) and the back-end ratio (all debt as a percentage of gross income) — so your estimate reflects what lenders will actually approve. First-time buyers frequently discover that their intuitive budget is either too conservative or, more dangerously, too aggressive once all costs are factored in. This calculator surfaces both limits so you can target the right range before speaking with a lender.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Annual gross income — Enter your total pre-tax household income for the year.
  2. Down payment — Enter the dollar amount you have saved for a down payment.
  3. Monthly debt payments — Include all recurring obligations: car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, and any other installment debt.
  4. Interest rate — Use a current 30-year fixed rate from a lender quote or a site like Freddie Mac's weekly survey.
  5. Loan term and property tax rate — Select your preferred term and enter your target area's annual property tax rate as a percentage of home value.
  6. Click Calculate — Results show the maximum home price under both the 28% and 36% rules, plus a recommended conservative ceiling.

Understanding Your Results

The 28% rule caps your monthly housing cost — principal, interest, property tax, and insurance (PITI) — at 28% of gross monthly income. The 36% rule caps all monthly debt including housing at 36% of gross income. When these two results differ, take the lower figure as your practical ceiling. If your existing debt load is high, the back-end rule will constrain you more than the front-end. If you carry little debt, the front-end rule likely binds. In either case, buying at the absolute maximum your lender approves leaves no financial buffer for maintenance, which typically costs 1–2% of the home's value per year, or for income disruptions.

Example Calculation

A couple earns $110,000 combined gross annual income ($9,167/month). They have $500/month in car payments and $200/month in student loan minimums — $700 total existing debt. They have saved $40,000 for a down payment. At a 6.75% interest rate on a 30-year loan with a 1.2% annual property tax rate: the 28% rule allows a maximum PITI of $2,567/month, supporting a loan of roughly $350,000 and a home price near $390,000. The 36% rule allows $3,300/month total debt, so $2,600/month for housing after the $700 existing debt, which supports a slightly higher loan. The conservative recommended ceiling falls around $390,000 — meaningfully lower than what an aggressive lender might offer.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between the 28% and 36% rules?

The 28% rule restricts your monthly housing payment (PITI) to 28% of gross monthly income. The 36% rule restricts all monthly debt obligations — housing plus car loans, student loans, and credit cards — to 36% of gross monthly income. Lenders evaluate both simultaneously, and you must satisfy whichever is more restrictive given your debt profile. Borrowers with substantial non-housing debt often find the 36% rule is the binding constraint.

What happens if I put down less than 20%?

A down payment below 20% on a conventional loan triggers Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI), which typically costs 0.5–1.5% of the loan balance per year. On a $350,000 loan that adds roughly $145–$438 per month until you reach 20% equity. FHA loans require mortgage insurance for the life of the loan regardless of equity. Down payment assistance programs exist in most states for first-time buyers and can provide grants or forgivable second mortgages to help reach a higher down payment threshold.

How much does existing debt reduce my home buying power?

Under the 36% back-end rule, every $100 of monthly debt reduces your maximum allowable housing payment by $100. Since mortgage payments correlate to loan size, a $500/month car payment can reduce your maximum loan by roughly $90,000–$100,000 depending on the interest rate and term. Paying off high-balance installment debt before applying for a mortgage is one of the most impactful steps a buyer can take to increase their purchase ceiling.

Should I buy at the top of what the calculator shows?

Financial planners generally recommend targeting a payment closer to 25% of gross income rather than the 28–36% lender maximum, to preserve cash flow for maintenance, savings, and emergencies. A home at the very top of your affordability range leaves little room if interest rates rise on a variable loan, if income drops, or if an expensive repair arises. Targeting 10–15% below your calculated maximum creates a meaningful financial cushion.

Does this calculator account for all homeownership costs?

This calculator estimates PITI — principal, interest, property taxes, and insurance. It does not include HOA fees (which can add $200–$600/month in many markets), routine maintenance (budget 1–2% of home value annually), utilities, or capital improvements. A $350,000 home may require an additional $3,500–$7,000 per year in maintenance alone. Factor these into your total housing budget rather than relying solely on the mortgage payment figure.

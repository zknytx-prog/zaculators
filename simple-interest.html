What Is the Simple Interest Calculator?

Simple interest is the most straightforward form of interest calculation: you earn or owe a fixed percentage of the original principal for each period, without any compounding. The formula is Interest = Principal × Rate × Time (I = PRT). Unlike compound interest — where interest earns interest on top of itself — simple interest is linear and predictable. It is commonly used for short-term personal loans, auto loans with simple interest structures, savings bonds, and some student loans during grace periods. Understanding how simple interest works gives you a baseline for comparing lending and savings products and helps you identify when a lender is using simple versus compound methods, which can significantly affect your total cost or return over time.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Principal — The original amount borrowed or invested before any interest is applied. For a loan, this is the amount you receive. For savings, it is the initial deposit.
  2. Annual interest rate (%) — The stated yearly rate. Enter 5 for 5%. This is the nominal rate, not the APR, which may include fees on some products.
  3. Time period — The duration of the loan or investment. Enter this in years. For six months, enter 0.5. For 18 months, enter 1.5.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator outputs the total interest earned or owed and the final balance (principal plus interest). For borrowers, this is what you will repay in addition to the principal. For investors, it is the total return. Simple interest always grows in a straight line — double the time, double the interest. This is different from compound interest, where the growth curve accelerates. On a $10,000 loan at 6% simple interest over 3 years, you owe exactly $1,800 in interest regardless of how quickly you pay it down. In a compound scenario, interest would accrue on any unpaid interest balance, making the total potentially higher. Real-world auto loans often use simple interest daily, so paying early can reduce total interest.

Example Calculation

You deposit $5,000 in a savings account offering 4.25% simple interest per year for 2 years. Interest = $5,000 × 0.0425 × 2 = $425. Total balance at the end: $5,425. Compare that to a compound interest account at the same rate: $5,000 × (1.0425)² = $5,436.80. The difference is small over two years but grows substantially over longer periods. For a $20,000 car loan at 7% simple interest over 5 years: Interest = $20,000 × 0.07 × 5 = $7,000. Total repayment = $27,000 before any early payment credit.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between simple and compound interest?

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal, so the interest amount is the same each period. Compound interest is calculated on both the principal and any previously accumulated interest, causing the balance to grow faster over time. Most savings accounts, mortgages, and credit cards use compound interest, while some personal loans and auto loans use simple interest daily accrual methods.

Does paying a simple interest loan early save money?

Yes, significantly. With daily simple interest loans — common in auto financing — interest accrues each day on the outstanding principal. Every extra payment you make reduces the principal balance, which reduces the daily interest charge going forward. Paying even $50 extra per month on a $15,000 auto loan can save hundreds of dollars and shorten the loan term by several months.

Is the rate I enter the same as APR?

Not always. The Annual Percentage Rate (APR) includes fees and other costs of borrowing beyond the stated interest rate, so it is always equal to or higher than the nominal rate. For a pure simple interest calculation, use the stated rate. To compare the true cost of different loan products, use their APRs, which lenders are required to disclose under the Truth in Lending Act.

Can I use this for Treasury bills or savings bonds?

Treasury bills use a discount yield calculation that is slightly different from straight simple interest, but the result is very close for short maturities. Series I savings bonds compound semiannually, so this calculator underestimates their return. For T-bills under one year, enter the discount rate and time fraction for a reasonable approximation of your interest earned.

How does simple interest apply to student loans in a grace period?

Federal unsubsidized student loans accrue simple interest during school and the six-month grace period after graduation. This accrued interest is then capitalized — added to the principal — when repayment begins, creating a larger balance. Making interest-only payments during school or the grace period prevents capitalization and reduces the long-term cost of the loan considerably.

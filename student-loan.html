What Is the Student Loan Calculator?

The Student Loan Calculator estimates your monthly payment and total repayment cost based on loan amount, interest rate, and repayment term. Student loans are the second-largest category of consumer debt in the United States, with over $1.7 trillion outstanding. Federal student loans and private student loans differ significantly in interest rates, repayment flexibility, and borrower protections. Federal direct subsidized loans for undergraduates carry rates set by Congress annually (6.53% for 2024–25); unsubsidized rates are the same but interest accrues during school. Graduate PLUS and parent PLUS loans carry higher rates (9.08% for 2024–25). Private loans vary by lender and creditworthiness, typically ranging from 4% to 16% APR. Understanding your total repayment cost before borrowing is the most powerful way to avoid over-borrowing and post-graduation financial stress.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Loan amount — Enter the total amount you expect to borrow. Use your school's financial aid award letter as a reference. Borrow only what you need — the difference between borrowing $25,000 and $35,000 at 6.5% over 10 years is roughly $110/month and $13,000 in total interest.
  2. Annual interest rate (%) — Enter the rate for your loan type. Check your loan servicer, the Federal Student Aid website, or your private lender's disclosure. Use the actual rate, not an APR that includes fees.
  3. Repayment term (years) — The standard federal repayment term is 10 years. Income-driven repayment plans extend this to 20 or 25 years with lower monthly payments but higher total interest. Extended repayment plans offer 25-year terms for balances over $30,000.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator shows your estimated monthly payment and total amount repaid over the loan term. The difference between total repaid and original principal is the total interest cost — this number often surprises borrowers. A $30,000 loan at 6.5% over 10 years carries a $339/month payment and $10,680 in total interest. Stretching the same loan to 25 years drops the monthly payment to $202 but increases total interest to $30,600 — more than doubling the interest cost. Income-driven repayment (IDR) plans like SAVE, IBR, PAYE, and ICR set payments at 5–10% of discretionary income and offer forgiveness after 20–25 years of qualifying payments, making them valuable for low-income borrowers or those pursuing Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF).

Example Calculation

A student borrows $28,000 in federal unsubsidized loans at 6.53% over the standard 10-year term. Monthly payment: approximately $317. Total repaid: $38,040. Total interest: $10,040. If this student qualifies for Public Service Loan Forgiveness after 10 years of payments on an IBR plan — making payments of roughly $150–200/month on a starting salary of $45,000 — the remaining balance could be forgiven tax-free, potentially saving $15,000–20,000 compared to standard repayment. PSLF requires full-time employment at a qualifying nonprofit or government employer and 120 qualifying payments.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between federal and private student loans?

Federal loans offer fixed rates set by Congress, income-driven repayment plans, deferment and forbearance protections, and access to forgiveness programs like PSLF. Private loans are issued by banks and credit unions, typically require a credit check or cosigner, may offer variable rates, and lack federal protections. Always exhaust federal borrowing limits before taking private loans, as federal loans offer substantially more borrower-friendly terms.

What is Public Service Loan Forgiveness?

PSLF cancels the remaining balance on federal Direct loans after 120 qualifying monthly payments while working full-time for a qualifying employer — federal, state, local, or tribal government, or a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Payments must be made under an income-driven repayment plan. The forgiven amount is tax-free under current law. Teachers, nurses, social workers, and government employees are common beneficiaries. Use the PSLF Help Tool at studentaid.gov to check employer eligibility.

How do income-driven repayment plans work?

IDR plans cap your monthly payment at a percentage of your discretionary income — typically 5–10% under SAVE (the newest plan), 10% under PAYE and new IBR, or 20% under ICR. After 20 or 25 years of qualifying payments (10 years under PSLF), the remaining balance is forgiven. IDR is most valuable when your loan balance is high relative to your income. Recertify your income annually to keep your payment current.

Should I pay extra toward my student loans?

Extra payments on federal loans reduce your principal and total interest but do not advance your forgiveness timeline under IDR or PSLF — qualifying payments are counted by number, not dollar amount. If you are pursuing forgiveness, extra payments may actually work against you. If you are on standard repayment with no forgiveness goal, extra payments save meaningful interest, especially in the first few years when most of each payment goes to interest.

Can I refinance federal student loans?

You can refinance federal loans into a private loan through a bank or credit union, often at a lower interest rate if you have good credit and steady income. However, refinancing permanently converts your federal loans to private loans, eliminating access to IDR plans, PSLF, federal deferment options, and any future federal forgiveness programs. Refinancing makes sense primarily for borrowers with high-rate private loans or those certain they will not use federal protections.

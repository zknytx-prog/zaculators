Understanding Tipping: A Practical US Guide

Standard Tipping Rates by Service Type

Tipping customs vary by industry and context. These are the widely recognized norms in the United States:

  • Sit-down restaurants: 18%–20% for standard service; 22%–25% for exceptional service. 15% is generally considered acceptable for merely adequate service but falls below current expectations at most full-service restaurants.
  • Food delivery: $3–$5 minimum for nearby orders; 15%–20% of the order total for larger orders, especially in bad weather or for long distances. Drivers bear vehicle expenses and often earn below minimum wage without tips.
  • Hair salons and barbershops: 15%–20% of the service cost. Many stylists are booth renters who keep 100% of tips — the tip is their primary variable income.
  • Hotel housekeeping: $2–$5 per night, left in a visible location with a note indicating it is for housekeeping. Daily tipping ensures the right person receives it, rather than a single checkout tip.
  • Rideshare and taxi: 15%–20% of the fare. Cash tips avoid app processing fees and are always appreciated.
  • Bartenders: $1–$2 per drink for simple orders; 15%–20% of the total tab for complex cocktails or table service.
  • Spa services: 15%–20% of the treatment cost. Tip in cash when possible so it goes directly to your service provider.

Pre-Tax vs. Post-Tax Tipping

There is a genuine debate about whether to tip on the pre-tax subtotal or the post-tax total. Tipping on the pre-tax amount is technically the traditional standard — you are rewarding the service, not the government's cut. Tipping on the post-tax total is simpler and has become increasingly common.

The practical difference is very small: on a $60 bill with 8% tax, the difference between 20% pre-tax and 20% post-tax is under a dollar ($12.00 vs. $12.96). Tipping generously matters more than which base you choose.

A Real Bill-Splitting Example

A group of three friends has dinner. The pre-tax bill totals $86.40. They want to tip 18% and split the total evenly:

  • Tip amount: $86.40 × 0.18 = $15.55
  • Bill + tip: $86.40 + $15.55 = $101.95
  • Per person: $101.95 ÷ 3 = $33.98

If they tip on the post-tax total (8% tax = $6.91, post-tax total = $93.31), the 18% tip becomes $16.80 and each person owes $36.70. The per-person difference is about $2.72 — worth knowing, but unlikely to create conflict.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is tipping mandatory in the US?

Tipping is not legally required, but it is a deeply embedded social norm. Federal law allows tipped workers to be paid as little as $2.13 per hour, with tips expected to make up the difference to minimum wage. Not tipping in a sit-down restaurant is viewed as a significant social slight.

What does "tip pooling" or "tip out" mean?

Many restaurants require servers to share a portion of their tips with support staff such as bussers, food runners, bartenders, and hosts. Your server may keep only 60%–70% of tips after the tip-out. Tipping generously benefits the entire service team.

Should I tip differently for counter service or takeout?

Expectations are lower for counter service and takeout than for table service. A tip of 10%–15% or simply rounding up is common and appreciated. Skipping entirely is socially acceptable, though any amount left helps.

How do I calculate a tip quickly without a calculator?

10% of any amount is simply moving the decimal one place left. Double it for 20%. For 18%, take 20% and subtract 10% of that result: on a $75 bill, 20% = $15.00, minus $1.50 = $13.50 for an 18% tip.

Can I tip less for poor service?

Yes. While the baseline for sit-down dining is 18%–20%, a tip of 10%–15% signals dissatisfaction. If service was genuinely unacceptable, speaking with a manager is often more effective, since servers may not always connect a small tip to a specific problem.

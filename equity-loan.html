What Is the Home Equity Loan Calculator?

A home equity loan calculator estimates your monthly payment and total interest cost for borrowing against the equity you have built in your home. A home equity loan — sometimes called a second mortgage — lets you borrow a lump sum at a fixed interest rate, using your home as collateral. Because the loan is secured by real estate, rates are typically much lower than personal loans or credit cards. Homeowners use equity loans to fund major home improvements, consolidate high-interest debt, cover large medical expenses, or finance education costs. The calculator helps you compare loan amounts and terms side by side before applying, so you know exactly what the monthly obligation will look like and how much you will pay in total interest over the loan's life. This matters because even a modest rate difference — say 7.5% versus 9% — on a $40,000 loan means hundreds of dollars in savings each year.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Loan amount — Enter how much you want to borrow. Most lenders cap combined LTV at 80–85% of your home's value, so your maximum is roughly 80% of home value minus your existing mortgage balance.
  2. Interest rate (APR) — Enter the annual percentage rate offered by your lender. Home equity loan rates typically run 7–11% depending on credit score, LTV, and market conditions.
  3. Loan term — Enter the repayment period in years. Common terms are 5, 10, 15, or 20 years. Longer terms lower the monthly payment but increase total interest paid.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator shows your fixed monthly payment and total interest paid over the full term. A shorter term dramatically reduces total interest — a $50,000 loan at 8.5% costs about $6,200 in interest over 5 years but $25,400 over 20 years. Choose the shortest term your budget can comfortably support. Also consider that your home is collateral: defaulting on a home equity loan puts your property at risk of foreclosure, unlike defaulting on a credit card. Verify the lender's closing costs (typically 2–5% of the loan amount) before finalizing, as these affect the true all-in cost. Add closing costs to your interest total to get an accurate picture of what the loan actually costs you.

Example Calculation

A homeowner wants to borrow $45,000 for a kitchen renovation. Lender A offers 8.25% APR over 10 years; Lender B offers 9.0% APR over 15 years. Lender A: monthly payment $551, total interest $21,120. Lender B: monthly payment $456, total interest $37,080. Lender B's monthly payment is $95 lower, but the homeowner pays $15,960 more in interest over the loan's life. If the budget can handle the extra $95/month, Lender A is clearly the better financial choice despite the higher monthly commitment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What credit score do I need for a home equity loan?

Most lenders require a minimum credit score of 620 for a home equity loan, but the best rates go to borrowers with scores of 740 or higher. Lenders also evaluate your combined loan-to-value ratio, debt-to-income ratio (ideally under 43%), and payment history. A score between 620–679 may qualify you for a loan but at a significantly higher interest rate — improving your score before applying can save thousands in interest.

Is interest on a home equity loan tax deductible?

Under current IRS rules, home equity loan interest is deductible only when the funds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home that secures the loan. Using the funds for debt consolidation, medical bills, or education does not qualify for the deduction. The deduction is only available if you itemize on Schedule A rather than taking the standard deduction. Consult a tax advisor for your specific situation.

Home equity loan vs. HELOC — which is better?

Choose a home equity loan when you need a specific lump sum for a defined purpose and want a fixed monthly payment for budgeting certainty. Choose a HELOC when you need flexible access to funds over time — like a renovation project where expenses are staggered — and can tolerate a variable interest rate. HELOCs often have lower initial rates but fluctuate with the prime rate; home equity loans lock in your rate on day one.

How long does it take to get a home equity loan?

The home equity loan process typically takes 2–6 weeks from application to funding. Lenders order an appraisal (or desktop appraisal), verify income and employment, check your credit, and review title. Gathering documents upfront — W-2s, pay stubs, mortgage statement, homeowner's insurance — speeds the process. Some online lenders and credit unions have streamlined the timeline to as fast as 1–2 weeks for well-qualified borrowers.

Can I pay off a home equity loan early?

Most home equity loans have no prepayment penalty, meaning you can make extra payments or pay off the balance entirely without a fee. Check your loan agreement to confirm this before signing. Paying extra principal each month significantly reduces your total interest cost and shortens the payoff timeline. Even an extra $100/month on a $45,000 loan at 8.5% can shave 2+ years off a 10-year term.

What Is the Debt Consolidation Calculator?

A debt consolidation calculator helps you figure out whether rolling multiple debts — credit cards, medical bills, personal loans — into one new loan makes financial sense. When you consolidate, you replace several high-rate balances with a single monthly payment, usually at a lower interest rate. The calculator compares what you currently pay across all debts against what you would pay under a consolidated loan, showing total interest saved, new monthly payment, and how long it will take to become debt-free. People most commonly use this tool when they feel overwhelmed by juggling several minimum payments, when a lender offers them a lower rate, or when they want a clear payoff date rather than an open-ended repayment drag. Lenders, credit counselors, and financial planners use the same math to explain options to clients. Running the numbers yourself first means you can walk into any conversation already knowing whether a consolidation offer is genuinely better or just superficially appealing.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Current balances — Enter the outstanding balance for each debt you want to consolidate. Pull these from your most recent statements.
  2. Current interest rates — Enter the annual percentage rate (APR) for each existing debt. Credit cards often run 20–29%, while personal loans may be 10–18%.
  3. Current monthly payments — Enter what you currently pay each month on each account.
  4. New loan interest rate — Enter the rate offered on the consolidation loan. Even a few percentage points lower can mean significant savings.
  5. New loan term — Choose how many months you want to take to repay. Shorter terms cost less in interest but raise the monthly payment.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator outputs a new monthly payment and total interest paid under the consolidated loan, then compares those figures to what you would spend continuing on your current path. A good result shows a lower monthly payment and meaningfully less total interest. However, a lower payment that comes only from stretching the term — not from a lower rate — may cost you more overall even though each check feels smaller. Pay attention to the total interest comparison, not just the monthly payment change. One limitation: the calculator assumes you stop adding to the debts being consolidated. If you consolidate credit cards and then run those balances back up, you end up worse off. Also note that consolidation loans often have origination fees of 1–8% of the loan amount; factor these in to get an accurate true cost comparison.

Example Calculation

Suppose you carry three debts: a $6,200 credit card at 24% APR, a $3,800 store card at 29% APR, and a $5,000 personal loan at 16% APR. Combined minimum payments run about $420 per month. A debt consolidation lender offers you a $15,000 personal loan at 11% APR over 48 months. Your new payment would be approximately $388/month — saving $32 each month — but more importantly, your total interest drops from roughly $5,900 (if you only paid minimums on the originals) to about $2,950 under the consolidation loan. That's nearly $3,000 in interest savings, and you have a fixed payoff date 4 years away instead of an indefinite rolling balance. The math clearly favors consolidation here.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does debt consolidation hurt your credit score?

Applying for a consolidation loan triggers a hard inquiry, which may drop your score a few points temporarily. However, paying down revolving balances lowers your credit utilization ratio, which typically boosts your score over time. Most people see a net positive effect within six to twelve months of consistently making on-time payments on the new loan.

What is the difference between debt consolidation and debt settlement?

Consolidation combines debts into one new loan you repay in full, at a lower rate. Settlement is when you negotiate with creditors to accept less than the full balance. Settlement can seriously damage your credit score, and forgiven amounts may be taxable income. Consolidation is generally the less risky path for people who can afford their payments but want a lower rate and simpler repayment structure.

Should I consolidate with a personal loan or a balance transfer card?

Balance transfer cards often offer 0% promotional rates for 12–21 months, which is unbeatable if you can pay off the balance before the promo ends. Personal loans work better for larger balances or longer repayment timelines where you need rate stability. Check for balance transfer fees (usually 3–5% of the amount moved) before assuming the card option is cheaper.

Can I consolidate student loans with personal debt?

Federal student loans should generally not be consolidated with personal loans, because doing so converts them to private debt and eliminates access to income-driven repayment plans, Public Service Loan Forgiveness, and federal forbearance programs. Private student loans can be refinanced together or combined with other private debts, but weigh those federal protections carefully before acting.

How do origination fees affect whether consolidation saves money?

Origination fees typically range from 1% to 8% of the loan amount and are often deducted from the proceeds or added to the balance. On a $15,000 loan with a 4% fee, you pay $600 upfront. Divide that fee by the monthly interest savings to find your break-even point. If you save $40/month in interest, you break even in 15 months — worthwhile for a 48-month loan but poor value on a short term.

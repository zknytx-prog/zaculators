What Is the Down Payment Calculator?

A down payment calculator helps you figure out exactly how much cash you need to buy a home — and how long it will take to save that amount given your current savings rate. The down payment is the portion of the purchase price you pay upfront; the rest is covered by a mortgage. The size of your down payment has a direct impact on your monthly payment, whether you pay private mortgage insurance (PMI), and how much interest you pay over the life of the loan. First-time buyers trying to understand how much they need to save, existing homeowners upgrading to a larger property, and anyone comparing conventional versus FHA loan options all rely on this type of calculator. Even a difference of 5 percentage points in your down payment — say, 10% versus 15% — can mean eliminating PMI and saving hundreds of dollars per month.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Home purchase price — Enter the target price of the home you want to buy, or the price range you're exploring.
  2. Down payment percentage — Enter your target down payment as a percentage. Common benchmarks are 3% (FHA minimum), 5%, 10%, and 20% (avoids PMI on conventional loans).
  3. Current savings — Enter how much you have saved specifically for a down payment right now.
  4. Monthly savings contribution — Enter how much you can add to your down payment fund each month going forward.
  5. Annual savings interest rate — Enter the yield on your savings account or HYSA. Even at 4–5%, this meaningfully shortens your savings timeline.

Understanding Your Results

The calculator outputs the total dollar amount needed and the number of months until you reach your goal. A 20% down payment eliminates PMI on conventional loans, which typically costs 0.5–1.5% of the loan amount per year — on a $350,000 loan, that's $1,750–$5,250 annually. If 20% is too far away, 10% down with lender-paid PMI or an 80/10/10 piggyback loan structure may be worth exploring. Also account for closing costs (typically 2–5% of the purchase price) and a 3–6 month emergency fund that should remain untouched after buying. Many buyers arrive at closing day having drained their savings, leaving them financially fragile for months afterward.

Example Calculation

A couple targets a $385,000 home and wants a 10% down payment of $38,500. They currently have $14,200 saved and contribute $900/month to a high-yield savings account earning 4.5% annually. The calculator shows they need $24,300 more. At $900/month with 4.5% interest, they reach their goal in approximately 26 months — about 2 years 2 months. They then also need to budget $7,700–$19,250 for closing costs, so their true all-in savings target is closer to $46,200–$57,750, extending the timeline by another 12–15 months.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum down payment for a conventional loan?

Most conventional loans require a minimum of 3% down for first-time buyers and 5% for repeat buyers. However, putting down less than 20% on a conventional loan requires private mortgage insurance (PMI). FHA loans require 3.5% down with a credit score of 580 or higher, or 10% down with scores between 500–579. VA and USDA loans offer 0% down options for qualifying borrowers.

Is it always better to put 20% down?

Not necessarily. In a fast-rising market, waiting years to save 20% while home prices climb can cost more than PMI would have. PMI is also now tax-deductible for many buyers and drops off automatically when equity reaches 20%. If your savings rate on the down payment funds is lower than your mortgage rate, it may make mathematical sense to put down less and invest the difference instead.

Can I use gift money for a down payment?

Yes. Most loan types allow gift funds from family members for the down payment, but lenders require a signed gift letter stating the money is not a loan that must be repaid. FHA loans allow 100% of the down payment to come from gifts. Conventional loans may require the buyer to contribute a portion of their own funds if the down payment is below a certain threshold, depending on the loan program.

What first-time homebuyer programs can reduce the down payment I need?

Many state housing finance agencies offer down payment assistance programs (DPAs) providing grants or zero-interest second mortgages of 3–5% of the purchase price. HUD-approved programs vary by state and county. Additionally, first-time buyers can withdraw up to $10,000 from a traditional IRA penalty-free for a home purchase, though income taxes still apply on the withdrawn amount.

Where should I keep my down payment savings?

Down payment funds you need within 1–3 years should stay in low-risk, liquid accounts. High-yield savings accounts (HYSAs) currently paying 4–5% APY are the most popular choice. Treasury bills or short-term CDs with no early withdrawal penalty are also suitable. Avoid investing down payment money in stocks — a market downturn right before you need the funds could delay your purchase by years.

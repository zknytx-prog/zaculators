What Is the BMR Calculator?

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is the number of calories your body burns every day just to keep you alive — breathing, circulating blood, maintaining body temperature, repairing cells, and running every other basic function — while you are completely at rest. It is the floor of your daily calorie needs, the minimum your body requires even if you do nothing all day. BMR is not the same as your total daily calorie need; it is the starting point before accounting for physical activity. Knowing your BMR helps you understand how many calories you burn at baseline, which is the foundation for setting realistic calorie targets whether your goal is weight loss, maintenance, or muscle gain. This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is widely considered the most accurate general-population BMR formula for adults.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Sex — Select male or female. The Mifflin-St Jeor equation uses different constants for each sex to account for average differences in lean body mass.
  2. Weight (lb) — Enter your current body weight in pounds. The calculator converts to kilograms internally for the formula.
  3. Height (inches) — Enter your height in total inches. For example, 5 feet 8 inches = 68 inches. The calculator converts to centimeters.
  4. Age — Enter your age in years. BMR decreases slightly with age as lean muscle mass tends to decline over time.
  5. Click Calculate — Results show your estimated daily BMR in kilocalories and your weekly resting calorie total.

Understanding Your Results

Your BMR result is your estimated resting daily energy expenditure. To get your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) — the actual number of calories you need per day — multiply your BMR by an activity factor: 1.2 for sedentary (desk job, little exercise), 1.375 for lightly active (exercise 1–3 days/week), 1.55 for moderately active (exercise 3–5 days/week), 1.725 for very active (hard exercise 6–7 days/week), and 1.9 for extremely active (physical job plus intense training). Never eat fewer calories than your BMR for extended periods without medical supervision — doing so can trigger muscle loss, metabolic adaptation, nutrient deficiencies, and other health problems. BMR estimates vary from actual measured values by roughly ±10% even with the best formulas.

Example Calculation

A 35-year-old woman, 5 feet 5 inches tall (65 inches), weighing 145 pounds. Converting: 145 lbs × 0.4536 = 65.8 kg; 65 inches × 2.54 = 165.1 cm. Mifflin-St Jeor for females: (10 × 65.8) + (6.25 × 165.1) − (5 × 35) − 161 = 658 + 1,031.9 − 175 − 161 = 1,353.9 kcal/day. Her BMR is approximately 1,354 calories per day. If she is moderately active, her TDEE is 1,354 × 1.55 ≈ 2,099 calories per day — the amount she would need to eat to maintain her current weight.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR is the calories your body burns at complete rest — the baseline energy for survival functions only. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) adds all other calorie use on top of BMR: the energy burned through digestion (about 10% of intake), non-exercise activity like walking and fidgeting, and structured exercise. TDEE is the number you actually need to eat at for weight maintenance; BMR is simply the floor that TDEE builds on.

Why does BMR decrease with age?

Lean muscle mass is metabolically active — it burns more calories at rest than fat tissue. Most adults lose roughly 3–8% of muscle mass per decade after age 30, a process called sarcopenia. As muscle mass decreases, BMR falls with it. This is one major reason why calorie intake patterns that maintained weight at 25 may lead to gradual weight gain by age 45 without changes to diet or exercise. Resistance training helps slow muscle loss and maintain a higher BMR over time.

Does eating less lower your BMR?

Yes. Significant calorie restriction causes metabolic adaptation, sometimes called "adaptive thermogenesis," where the body reduces energy expenditure to preserve itself. Studies show BMR can drop 10–20% below predicted values during prolonged calorie deficits. This is a key reason why very low calorie diets become less effective over time and why reintroducing food after a diet is often complicated. Gradual, moderate deficits (300–500 calories/day below TDEE) cause less adaptation than extreme restriction.

Which BMR formula is most accurate — Mifflin-St Jeor or Harris-Benedict?

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation, published in 1990, is generally considered more accurate for most modern adults than the original Harris-Benedict equations from 1919. Multiple validation studies show Mifflin-St Jeor predicts measured resting metabolic rate within about 10% for roughly 80% of people. The Katch-McArdle formula, which uses lean body mass, can be more accurate for individuals who know their body fat percentage accurately, but requires that additional measurement.

How should I use my BMR result for weight loss?

Start by calculating your TDEE (BMR × activity factor). Then create a moderate deficit of 300–500 calories below your TDEE per day to lose approximately 0.6–1 pound per week. Avoid going below your BMR in daily calorie intake, as this risks muscle loss and metabolic slowdown. Recheck your BMR every 10–15 pounds lost, since BMR decreases as body weight decreases and your targets need to adjust accordingly.

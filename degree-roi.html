What Is the Degree ROI Calculator?

A degree ROI (return on investment) calculator measures whether the financial cost of a college degree is justified by the lifetime earnings boost it produces. It works like any other investment analysis: you put in money upfront (tuition, fees, lost wages during school), and you expect greater income over your career as a result. The calculator takes your total education costs, the number of years you plan to work, and the expected salary difference between having the degree and not having it, then outputs a payback period and total lifetime gain or loss. Students deciding between schools, adults weighing whether to go back to school, and families comparing in-state versus out-of-state options all use this kind of analysis. Because a degree can cost anywhere from $20,000 at a community college to $300,000 at a private university, even a modest income premium can look very different depending on the price tag you're working with.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Total education cost — Enter all-in costs: tuition, fees, books, and room and board for the full program. Use net cost after grants, not sticker price.
  2. Years to complete degree — Enter how long you will be in school. Each extra year also means another year of foregone income.
  3. Salary without degree — Enter what you currently earn or expect to earn without the credential. This is your opportunity cost baseline.
  4. Expected salary with degree — Enter the median salary for your target field and degree level. Check the Bureau of Labor Statistics for current data.
  5. Years in workforce — Enter how many years you plan to work after graduating. Longer careers amplify the ROI of any degree.

Understanding Your Results

The key output is the payback period — how many years of working it takes to recoup the full cost of the degree, including foregone wages. After that break-even point, every additional year of work at the higher salary is pure gain. A payback period under 10 years is generally considered strong for a four-year degree. Beyond the payback period, the total lifetime earnings premium shows the true upside. Keep in mind this model uses constant dollar values and ignores salary growth, inflation, and loan interest. If you borrowed to attend school, factor in total interest paid as part of your true investment cost. Also, some degrees open doors to careers that simply do not exist without the credential — like medicine or law — making the income comparison a floor, not a ceiling.

Example Calculation

A student considers a four-year nursing degree costing $52,000 total (net of grants). During those four years she would have earned $14/hour full-time — about $29,120/year — totaling $116,480 in foregone wages. Her total investment is $52,000 + $116,480 = $168,480. With a BSN she expects to earn $72,000/year versus $34,000/year without the degree — a premium of $38,000/year. Divide $168,480 by $38,000 and the payback period is 4.4 years after graduation. Over a 35-year career, her total earnings premium is $38,000 × 35 = $1,330,000 — a very strong ROI for a $168,480 investment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the calculator account for student loan interest?

The basic calculator uses total cost as a lump sum and does not automatically add loan interest. To get an accurate picture, use your loan payoff calculator to find total interest paid over the repayment period, then add that figure to your education cost input. A $52,000 degree financed at 6.5% over 10 years carries about $18,000 in interest, making the true cost closer to $70,000.

Which majors have the highest return on investment?

Engineering, computer science, nursing, accounting, and applied mathematics consistently show the strongest ROI because their median salaries are high relative to the cost of the degree. Degrees in fine arts, general humanities, and some social sciences often show longer payback periods, though non-monetary value and career satisfaction are real factors the calculator cannot capture.

How does an associate degree compare to a bachelor's degree in ROI?

An associate degree at a community college can show excellent ROI because the cost is dramatically lower — often $8,000–$20,000 total — while the salary premium for skilled trades, dental hygiene, or radiology tech can be substantial. A two-year program that leads to a $55,000 salary may pay back faster than a $180,000 private bachelor's degree leading to a $65,000 salary.

Should I use sticker price or net price in the cost field?

Always use net price — tuition minus grants and scholarships you do not repay. The College Scorecard and each school's Net Price Calculator show average net costs by income level. Using sticker price will dramatically overstate your investment and make any degree look worse than it actually is. Loans count in your cost, grants do not.

How do I find accurate salary data for my target career?

The Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook lists median annual wages by occupation and provides 10-year job growth projections. For entry-level specifics, check the National Association of Colleges and Employers (NACE) salary surveys. LinkedIn Salary and Glassdoor offer real-world self-reported data that can complement the official figures when researching niche roles.

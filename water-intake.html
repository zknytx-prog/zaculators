Frequently Asked Questions

Why is hydration so important for health?

Water comprises 60% of adult body weight and is essential for: oxygen transport, temperature regulation, nutrient absorption, joint lubrication, toxin elimination, and cognitive function. Dehydrationâ€”even mild (2% body weight loss)â€”impairs physical performance, concentration, and mood. Chronic dehydration increases risk of kidney stones, UTIs, and constipation.

Is the "8x8 rule" (64 oz/day) accurate?

8x8 (eight 8-oz glasses = 2 liters) is oversimplified. Actual needs vary by weight, activity, climate. A 50kg person needs less than 100kg person. This calculator accounts for individual factors. "Drink when thirsty" is reasonable for most people, but thirst lags behind actual need (unreliable in heat/exercise). Urine color is better indicator than fixed amount.

Can you drink too much water?

Yesâ€”rare but serious. Hyponatremia (water intoxication) occurs when water intake overwhelms kidneys' excretion capacity, diluting blood sodium dangerously. Primarily affects endurance athletes drinking excessive water. Symptoms: confusion, headache, nausea, seizures. Safe practice: don't force excessive water beyond thirst; use sodium-containing drinks during prolonged exercise.

Do coffee and tea count toward daily water intake?

Yes, mostly. Caffeine causes mild diuretic effect (increases urination) but doesn't eliminate hydration benefit. Net fluid gain from caffeinated drinks is still positive. However, plain water is betterâ€”no added sugar or calories. Alcohol and excessive caffeine have stronger diuretic effects. Prioritize water but don't exclude other beverages entirely.

How do I know if I'm dehydrated?

Signs: dark urine, thirst, dry mouth, fatigue, headache, dizziness, reduced urination. In heat/exercise, check urine colorâ€”best hydration indicator. Thirst often lags behind actual dehydration. Chronic mild dehydration often goes unnoticed but impairs cognition and physical performance. Regular hydration schedule (preventive) better than waiting for thirst (reactive).

Does water before bed affect sleep?

Drinking too much water before bed can cause nighttime urination, disrupting sleep. Solution: consume most daily water during day, taper evening intake 2-3 hours before bed. This maintains daytime hydration while minimizing nighttime disruption. Older adults and those with urinary issues should especially minimize late evening fluid intake.
